Maintaining Network Cameras

The building I work in has over 200 cameras throughout and we are constantly installing cameras in new locations. This gives me two main jobs; managing the installation of new cameras and making sure the operators can view the cameras. When it comes to installing the cameras first comes location. This usually requires me to ask the quality group or the operators where they want cameras. Next it requires finding or installing spots to wire the cameras into our network. Finding solutions to provide the operators with standard definition coax based video and/or high definition ip based video is challenging and keeps me quite busy.

HMI Screen Development

Through my job I have also gained experience with using Visual Basic to create HMI screens. By developing HMI screens I had the oppourtunity to work on the front end and back end of the screen giving my valuable experience in interface design and taught me how to code in Visual Basic. One of the most important things I learned while working on interface design is just because its possible to do something does not mean it should be done. To elaborate, the picture shown is one of my earliest developed HMI screens. It shows numerous boxes with green and red backgrounds. Well, I'm not red-green color blind so to me I saw nothing wrong with using green for good and red for bad. Seemed pretty universally accepted to me. Well it was not until I talked to one of our senior HMI screen developers that he explained to me that some of our operators are red-green colorblind and would not be able to distinguish good for bad with my current method. He taught me its more accepted to not show any color for good things and show a flashing color for bad things. This way it would get the users attention regardless of disabilities.
